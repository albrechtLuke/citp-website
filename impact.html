Project Impact

Cultural Relevance

Author, A. A. (Year). *Title of image*. Website Name. URL

Cultural engagement and respect for the Saibai Island community are integral to the long-term success of this project. All of the Torres Strait Islands, including Saibai, have a deeply rooted cultural connection to the sea, with traditional fishing, navigation, and ocean stewardship forming the foundation of community identity. Therefore, introducing technological systems such as tide-sensing buoys and artificial reefs must be done in a way that aligns with the community’s values, practices, and governance structures under Ailan Kastom (Island Custom).


Ailan Kastom is not only a traditional system of law but a framework for the management of land and sea. It emphasises respect for the environment, shared responsibility, and the interdependence between Traditional Peoples and the ocean. The introduction of the tide monitoring and reef maintenance project upholds these principles by positioning the Saibai community as stewards and active custodians of the infrastructure. The project does not force an external solution, but instead integrates with traditional knowledge, ensuring that the system complements local customs and enhances existing practices of environmental care.


Through direct consultation with community elders and local governance bodies, the project ensures that cultural protocols are observed in all phases of design, construction, and implementation. Elders’ guidance will help determine the most suitable locations for the artificial reefs, avoiding areas of cultural significance, ancestral fishing grounds, or spiritual importance. This participatory approach reinforces the principle of self-determination, ensuring that the Saibai people are not passive recipients of technology, but active leaders in its design and management.


Additionally, the artificial reefs themselves serve a dual cultural and ecological role. By increasing the abundance of native shellfish, reef fish, and other marine life, they directly support traditional fishing and gathering practices. Under the Native Title Act 1993, the Saibai people retain the right to engage in cultural fishing for subsistence and ceremonial purposes. The deployment of the reefs, therefore, not only enhances food security but also strengthens cultural continuity, allowing younger generations to learn traditional fishing methods within a thriving and biodiverse marine environment.


The project also acknowledges the importance of intergenerational learning. The upskilling and training components allow knowledge to flow both ways—traditional ecological knowledge informs sustainable management practices, while technical maintenance and monitoring skills are passed on to community members. This exchange fosters cultural resilience, ensuring that technology serves as a bridge rather than a barrier between tradition and modernisation.


Ultimately, the cultural relevance of this project lies in its partnership model. It is not merely about installing equipment but about embedding these systems into the cultural fabric of Saibai life. By respecting community authority, valuing local insight, and aligning with Ailan Kastom, the project supports the community’s right to care for their land and sea on their own terms—upholding both cultural heritage and climate resilience.

Upskill Opportunities

A key pillar of this initiative is the commitment to capacity building and upskilling within the Saibai community. The project creates pathways for local residents to acquire technical, environmental, and mechanical skills that directly relate to maintaining tide-sensing buoys and artificial reefs.


Maintenance of marine infrastructure in remote areas typically requires costly external intervention. By training local residents to carry out regular inspections, calibration, and minor repairs, the project shifts ownership and expertise to the community itself. This hands-on learning approach will include modules on basic electronics, data transmission systems, buoyancy mechanics, corrosion prevention, and ecological monitoring. Training will also cover health and safety procedures, use of protective gear, and record-keeping for maintenance logs.

Elders’ guidance will help determine the most suitable locations for the artificial reefs, avoiding areas of cultural significance, ancestral fishing grounds, or spiritual importance. This participatory approach reinforces the principle of self-determination, ensuring that the Saibai people are not passive recipients of technology, but active leaders in its design and management.


In relation to the artificial reefs, participants will learn practical marine management techniques such as underwater cleaning, reef health assessments, biodiversity documentation, and sediment analysis. These skills can later be expanded into broader fields, including marine science assistance, eco-tourism, and fisheries management—industries that are becoming increasingly valuable across the Torres Strait.


The upskilling component aligns with local employment needs. Currently, Saibai Island faces limited economic opportunities, with a labour force participation rate of only 26.8%. By creating paid, community-based maintenance positions, this project introduces new sources of income while promoting skill development that remains relevant across the region. Furthermore, these skills are transferable—participants can later apply them in other marine sustainability projects or in collaboration with regional organisations such as the Torres Strait Regional Authority (TSRA) or the Australian Institute of Marine Science (AIMS).


Another key advantage of the upskill plan is its long-term sustainability. Rather than relying on short-term training programs, the project will establish a mentorship structure, where early trainees can become local trainers for future participants. This “train-the-trainer” model fosters self-sufficiency, ensuring that maintenance knowledge remains within the community for decades.


Importantly, the training program also incorporates traditional ecological knowledge. Local experts who have deep understanding of currents, tides, and marine species behaviour will be invited to share their insights alongside technical instructors. This two-way learning model respects Indigenous expertise as equal to scientific knowledge, creating a hybrid skillset that blends cultural understanding with modern technology.


By empowering Saibai residents with these abilities, the project enhances resilience not only to climate threats but also to economic vulnerability. It creates a foundation for community-led infrastructure management, where residents have the competence and confidence to maintain, troubleshoot, and improve their systems independently. In doing so, the project embodies principles of local empowerment, self-reliance, and long-term sustainability.

Environmental Impact

Author, A. A. (Year). *Title of image*. Website Name. URL

The environmental benefits of the project extend across several dimensions: coastal protection, biodiversity restoration, and climate adaptation.


The artificial reefs, constructed from cinderblocks, are designed to act as both ecological habitats and structural anchors for the tide-sensing buoys. Cinderblock materials, rich in calcium, promote the settlement and growth of marine invertebrates, shellfish, and corals by increasing local alkalinity. This chemical property helps counteract the effects of ocean acidification, a growing threat in the Torres Strait region. Over time, these reefs become living ecosystems that enhance marine biodiversity, attract fish populations, and restore habitats lost to coastal erosion.


Ecologically, the reefs serve as nursery grounds for many native species, contributing to the replenishment of fisheries and providing sustainable food sources for the community. As marine life establishes itself on the reef surfaces, a complex web of organisms—including algae, molluscs, crustaceans, and small reef fish—emerges, improving local ecosystem productivity.


From a coastal engineering perspective, the artificial reefs also provide a degree of shoreline protection. Positioned strategically along tidal pathways, they help dissipate wave energy, reduce the force of storm surges, and limit longshore drift that causes sediment loss. This passive form of protection reduces the pressure on man-made seawalls and other coastal defences, providing an environmentally friendly alternative to traditional hard engineering solutions.


The tide-sensing buoys contribute to environmental monitoring by providing continuous, real-time data on sea level fluctuations. This data can be used by local authorities and climate scientists to track the effects of rising sea levels, identify long-term trends, and improve regional adaptation planning. By generating high-resolution local data, the Saibai system fills a critical gap in Australia’s coastal monitoring network—especially for northern regions that currently lack reliable sensors.


Environmental sustainability is also built into the materials and energy systems of the project. The buoys are made from closed-cell polyethylene foam, chosen for its durability, buoyancy, and resistance to UV radiation and saltwater corrosion. Power is supplied through polycrystalline solar panels connected to lithium-ion batteries, ensuring that the system operates autonomously with zero emissions. Components such as the stainless-steel mooring chain and gearbox are designed for longevity and easy replacement, minimising material waste over time.


In addition, by training local residents to conduct regular maintenance and inspection, the project ensures that environmental degradation—such as corrosion, debris accumulation, or sensor fouling—is addressed promptly, extending the operational lifespan of both the reefs and buoys.


Overall, the project demonstrates a holistic environmental impact that combines conservation, adaptation, and education. It strengthens the natural resilience of Saibai’s marine ecosystem, supports biodiversity growth, and provides critical data to inform broader climate resilience strategies.
