The Tide Monitoring Challenge

Understanding the critical problems facing coastal monitoring today

Current Challenges

Author, A. A. (Year). *Title of image*. Website Name. URL

Topographic Challenges

Saibai island, located in the north-western Torres Strait, is highly vulnerable to storm tides, rising seas, and extreme weather events. With its highest point only 1.7 meters above sea level, even minor tidal surges can lead to severe flooding. Repeated inundation on the island has already caused significant erosion, damaged roads, seawalls, and community facilities, and threatens the long-term habitability of the island.

Lack of Monitoring Systems

Currently, the island does not have any reliable or active tidal monitoring on the island. The sole weather station on the island is offline, leaving no real-time data on sea levels, rainfall, or storm surges. Without this critical information, the community cannot predict hazardous events or issue timely warnings, leaving the residents of Saibai exposed and vulnerable to sudden flooding and storm impacts.

Poor Infrastructure

Existing infrastructure is aging, limited and unable to support the community independently during disasters. The island relies on a single 4G tower and diesel-generated power, both of which are extremely prone to failure under extreme weather conditions. This ultimately means that emergency alerts and communication can be lost exactly when they are needed most.

Current Practices in Place

Currently, residents of Saibai are informed of emergencies through communication via the government. As the primary form of alert, the Bureau of Meteorology (BOM) initially identifies the emergency, notifies the Queensland State Disaster Coordinator who then communicates it to the Cairns District Disaster Coordinator. This information is then passed to the Torres Strait Local Disaster Management Group and members of the Community Disaster Management Group. By the time the alert reaches residents, it is communicated through multiple channels, including in-person notifications, Emergency Alert SMS or landline messages, local radio broadcasts, official social media platforms, and the Yumi Safe Dashboard.

Issues with Current Practices

The biggest issue is that this system requires active human intervention and is susceptible to delays, especially during nighttime or adverse weather conditions, when king tides, typically occurring in winter, are most dangerous. It is further limited by network connectivity, which can face outages in Saibai due to the dependence on the 4G network, as well as power supply issues which can create additional outages during emergencies (EWB, 2025). The lack of reliable network connection is a major issue in communication warnings. Moreover, tidal behaviour can vary significantly even between nearby islands, meaning mainland-sourced data may not accurately reflect local conditions (Rainbird, 2016)

Broader Issues

Lack of Technology

Most existing monitoring equipments are not designed for remote island conditions, making setup and operation difficult

  • Systems too large or costly for small sites.
  • Limited technical support for local users.
  • Poor adaptability to tidal and coastal environments.

Communication Barriers

Weak network coverage limits the ability to transmit or access real-time data.

  • Unstable 4G connection.
  • Data delays during emergencies.
  • Weak email alerting system.

Limited Coverage

Most monitoring stations are sparsely distributed, creating gaps in coastal monitoring coverage and limiting data availability.

  • Large distances between stations
  • Remote locations with poor connectivity
  • Insufficient data for local conditions

Accuracy Problems

Environmental factors and equipment limitations can significantly impact measurement accuracy and reliability.

  • Wave interference with measurements
  • Temperature and pressure variations
  • Limited sensor redundancy

Maintenance Issues

Existing systems require frequent maintenance due to harsh marine environments, leading to data gaps and unreliable measurements.

  • Corrosion from saltwater exposure.
  • Biofouling affecting sensor accuracy.
  • Weather damage and vandalism.

Power supply instability

Monitoring systems depend on consistent and reliable energy sources, which can be challenging in remote island environments.

  • Diesel generators may run out during storms.
  • Harsh weather increases the risk of generator or battery failures.

Environmental Research

Limited data availability hampers climate change research, coastal erosion studies, and ecosystem monitoring efforts.

Coastal Communities

Inadequate monitoring puts communities at risk from flooding, storm surges, and other coastal hazards.

Maritime Operations

Shipping, fishing, and recreational boating activities are compromised by unreliable tide predictions.
